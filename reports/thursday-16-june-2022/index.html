Thursday 16 June 2022

Subscribe to the ADM Agriculture YouTube channel for the latest grain, seed & fertiliser market updates and much more!

Wheat

  • Over the past week the US market has continued to trade lower, down about $7/t, as continued improvement of new crop prospects helps to ease market pressures.
  • Last Friday saw the latest release of USDA’s global supply and demand figures. The June report typically contains gentle amendments at best, and this was no exception.
  • Key highlights were:
    • World wheat production was trimmed down 1.4 mln t to 773.43 mln t.
    • Despite trade expectations of a reduced US production figure, an increase to US yields saw a modest increase to total production of 47.26 mln t.
    • Russian wheat production was only nudged up 1 mln t to 81 mln t, still some way short of private analysts who are circling the 85-87 mln t figure (SovEcon leading the way at 88.6 mln t), with expected exports in the region of 42 mln t.
    • USDA expects Ukraine’s new crop corn output to rise by 4 mln t, the main contributor to the surprise jump in global corn ending stocks that are now posted at 310.45 mln t.
  • EU prices have not followed the US markets though, with new crop MATIF (Dec) and London (Nov) futures up €6/t and £7.5/t respectively over the period.
  • Talk continues of export corridors from Ukraine into the global market. However, progress is still slow and questions remain, with Turkey’s President Erdogan speaking to the Ukrainians this week.
  • Ukraine’s deputy agriculture minister estimated the 2022 grain harvest to be around 48.5 mln t compared with last year’s 86 mln t. The total area sown for the 2022 campaign is estimated to be down about 25% following Russia’s invasion. We talked to one former Ukrainian farm manager last week who was concerned that fires ignited by the fighting could threaten crops.
  • France’s wheat crop continues to prove worrisome as temperatures continue to soar, with crop ratings sliding again week on week.
  • FranceAgriMer reduced its estimates of French non-EU soft exports by 150,000t to 9.1 mln t for 2021/22, in light of fresh data showing a slowdown in export pace as the campaign comes to a close. Intra-EU numbers remained unchanged at 8 mln t for the year, while soft wheat ending stocks remained unchanged at 3.2 mln t.
  • The UK crop continues to look broadly good, as the recent showers/sunshine cycle has helped boost yield potential. Harvest start-date predictions vary nationally, with some talking of wheat being as much as 10 days ahead in some areas, although for the most part, expectations seem to be at the early end of normal.

Malting Barley

  • Global malting barley markets are very quiet, with the main buyer China out of the market.
  • EU buyers were in the market for most of last week and bought a reasonable amount of barley.
  • EU prices had dropped around €15, but have started to recover over the past few days due to hot and dry weather concerns for France.
  • UK buyers were also in the market last week and prices came down by £5 to £10/t.
  • Like the EU, UK prices have started to rise again in the last few days.
  • UK FOB continues to be a £20/£30 discount to the domestic market.
  • ADM crop 2023 buyback terms are now available with plenty of different contracting options.

Feed Barley

  • Old crop barley sellers have started to appear in recent weeks as contracts are cleared and balances become known. However, old crop positions are now completely covered by both trade and consumption. As a result, the old crop/new crop inverse has been completely eroded and any remaining stocks are trading at harvest levels.
  • Globally, barley trade has been very thin, with demand watching from the sidelines. FOB prices in Romania are starting to come under pressure as harvest gets underway in the Black Sea.
  • After a flurry of activity for harvest last week, the Spanish market has been rather quiet this week. Nevertheless, the UK should see good demand from Spain going forward into the new season, with declines in production forecast. The UK is still the cheapest FOB origin in northern Europe.
  • Consumers in the UK market are maintaining a risk-off approach and there is very little activity day-to-day.
  • Farmers in the UK are sold ahead of usual pace, and farmer selling feels slow as a result. Both the crops and the forecast look promising for a good harvest.

Rapeseed

  • At the end of last week USDA’s report reduced US soybean ending stocks below trade expectations. But world ending stocks for 2021/22 soybeans were actually reported higher than trade estimates at 86.15 mln t (average estimate: 84.82 mln t), and 2022/23 ending stocks were reported at 100.46 mln t vs estimates at 99.77 mln t.
  • Soybean prices traded off highs this week, dipping below $17. Informa estimates the US bean area at 88.7 mln acres vs USDA’s 90.9 mln acres, with some farmers still to finish. US weather still shows hot and dry weather for the next few weeks, which may pressure crop conditions, although crop ratings earlier this week were reported at 70% good/excellent, in line with estimates.
  • In China, it was reported that traders cancelled 100,000mt of US purchases and switched to Brazilian supplies. There are still concerns over some areas going back into lockdown which may knock product demand.
  • USDA estimates Brazil’s soybean crop at 126 mln t vs 139.5 mln t last week. Argentinian soybean production was estimated at 43.4 mln t vs 46.2 mln t last year.
  • Brazilian farmers are nearly fully sold and clearing out the bins ready for their corn harvest.  In Argentina, farmer selling has risen in the past few weeks to 17.9 mln t, but remains behind last year. It was rumoured that both Argentina and Brazil will increase their biofuel mandates in the not-too-distant future, which will send support to veg oil prices.
  • Crude oil prices fell off recent highs, with West Texas Intermediate trading $3 down in yesterday’s session, $7 off recent highs.
  • Veg oil prices are still in a downward trend, pressured at the start of the week with Indonesia speeding up the export pace of palm oil in addition to lowering export taxes. Parts of China are going back into lockdown and energy prices were pressured, which had a knock on-effect into veg oils.
  • Canadian canola and MATIF rapeseed tried to bounce from the sell-off at the start of the week, but struggled to break nearby resistance levels without anything new to trade on.
  • For the UK, sterling was the big story, as it fell to lows against the euro and dollar not seen since the start of 2021. Investors seem to have lost confidence in the UK economy, with inflation rising and the Bank of England struggling to control it. Levels have since partly recovered.

Oats

  • Oat markets remain largely quiet, with EU buyers looking to buy new crop significantly below levels offered by some traditional exporters. Over the course of the next few months those prices will connect as consumers look to hedge business that is historically written in Sept/Oct.
  • Parts of the EU will see temperatures exceed 30°C this week, which could have a negative impact on yield if they linger for any length of time. Currently there is no significant selling pressure from producers therefore prices remain supported. However, with the old crop market being very tight buyers will be keen to see how the new crop performs. Any quality issue in the EU could see prices rise.
  • Here in the UK, the weather remains largely favourable. It is however too early to say whether we are going to have a bumper crop or not, but a production figure of around 1 mln t is currently being estimated. If realised, we will need to be competitive into Europe. Feed oats remain of interest into the feed compound sector, so we should expect another year of usage above the five-year average.
  • Bottom line, it is still all to play for.

Pulses

Beans

  • Old crop feed bean demand is extremely thin and we have seen the inverse completely collapse, leading the prices between old and new to be at the same level.
  • New crop beans have seen excellent conditions in the fields for pulse production, with most pulse crops looking very well at this stage.
  • Farmer selling is still very thin on new crop as is demand. Beans are looking expensive versus other mid-range proteins.

Peas

  • Old crop peas remain quiet, with no farmer selling, and domestically companies are covered in the nearby positions. Demand for human consumption peas into new crop is ramping up. Export-wise on feed peas there appears to be some demand heading into next season.
  • 2023 marrowfat buyback is likely to land next week. Please keep an eye out and discuss with your farm buyer.

Seed

 

  • We have variety demonstration days taking place across the country throughout June. It is not too late to get booked onto a variety day close to you. Contact your farm trader for more information.
  • Our autumn seed availability currently looks good across most mainstream varieties, although we are now beginning to sell out of some of the newly recommended varieties. If you have seen something you like at Cereals or at one of our trials days, please get in touch sooner rather than later to secure tonnage for this autumn.
  • We still have a range of Countryside Stewardship mixes available to suit a variety of schemes.
  • Cover cropping is a great option for helping to manage the soils, diseases and weeds – we have a great selection of cover crop straights. Contact your farm trader for more information.
  • As we work our way towards July, it is a great time to start thinking about companion crops for OSR. We have three options to choose from, which will help aid establishment of the crop. For more information view our seed varieties information guide.

Fertiliser

  • US prices (NOLA) have slipped due to regional demand concerns for nitrogen and traders chasing values that calculate to re-export to other markets such as India and Brazil.
  • North African FOB prices are remaining supported by Europe, which continues to be a premium market due to regional production costs.
  • UK granular urea continues to trade at a discount to ammonium nitrate (AN) and urea ammonium nitrate (UAN) products on a £/kg basis. We have all delivery windows to suit your needs for crop 22/23.
  • UK natural gas prices jumped about 9% this morning, hitting highs not seen since March/April 2022, when UK AN prices were between £785-915/t.
  • UK AN terms for spot delivery remain available on a limited tonnage basis at present.
  • Liquid UAN prices remain available for summer tank fill. Spring fill terms are not yet available.
