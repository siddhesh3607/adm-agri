Thursday 21 July 2022

Wheat

  • Markets have been mixed on the week as macro-economics, weather and Black Sea uncertainty create a fluctuating market.
  • Chicago wheat futures (Dec22) edged up $2.76/t, but Paris slipped €7/t and London (Nov 22) lost £7.30/t.
  • Global wheat prospects remain mixed. Russia’s IKAR agricultural consultancy has raised its wheat production estimate for the country to 90.5 mln t, up 1.8 mln t on its previous estimate. Export potential is put at 45 mln t.
  • French farmers had harvested 50% of this year’s soft wheat crop as of 11 July, with reports of much better crops in the north and east of the country.
  • Early reports from Germany shows an optimistic outlook for yield and quality in this year’s wheat harvest, offsetting earlier concerns from some farm groups.
  • The recent heatwave has allowed UK farmers to start harvesting new crop wheat, and although early results show much variation on yields, quality so far appears to be very good.
  • Conversely, Canadian traders report that crops are still behind their normal stage of development due to adverse weather conditions across many of the provinces.
  • In addition, Rosario Grain exchange now sees Argentina’s 2022 wheat production at 17.7 mln t, from a previous estimate of 18.5 mln t, citing lower plantings and dry weather for the decline.
  • Buenos Aires Grain Exchange kept Argentina’s 2022 wheat planting area unchanged week on week at 6.2 mln ha.
  • Talk of an export corridor from Ukraine remains just that. President Putin has said establishing such an export corridor would depend on need additional lifting of restrictions on Russian exports, including finance, payment, freight and insurance.
  • However, analysts report that the Russian-occupied peninsula of Crimea is shipping more than 50 times the volume of grain it usually does at this time of the year, raising suspicions that seized Ukrainian grain is being taken abroad.
  • The EU has decided to amend Russian sanctions by unfreezing funds in seven Russian banks, provided they can prove the funds are being used for agricultural products.
  • Chinese customs data showed June’s wheat imports of 520,000t, down 31% year on year, with the year-to-date imports reported at 4.94 mln t, down 8%.
  • Egypt’s state buyer GASC is in private talks with several companies to import wheat. It has not purchased any tonnage in its latest tender due to high prices amid limited offers.
  • Meanwhile Pakistan has purchased 300,000t of wheat at an international tender, with another 200,000t tender due to be issued on 25 July.

Malting Barley

  • Overall the quality of the winter crop has remained excellent throughout harvest and nitrogen levels are low across the board, which is adding harvest pressure to the spot winter barley market.
  • The promising start to harvest has put buyers at ease, and demand is currently extremely slow across the board. Buyers are waiting to see if the spring quality mirrors that of the winter crop, which is the current expectation from the market.
  • FOB markets are non-existent and we see sellers offering into a void across Europe.
  • If the quality of the spring crop is sufficient, given the current demand outlook we expect to see malting premiums push lower from their current level of circa £70/t.

Feed Barley

  • The winter barley harvest is almost now complete following good conditions over the last few weeks. Markets continue to push lower in the spot position as sellers look for outlets to move as-available contracts. We retain our yield estimation of 10% over average levels for the winter crop and quality overall remains excellent.
  • Demand is disappointingly slow from destinations, with Spanish interest all but disappearing from the market. The low water levels on the rivers in the Netherlands resulting from the recent heatwave has stimulated some demand as buyers look for coaster execution to ease logistics, however bids are few and far between.
  • Reductions in EU corn crop forecasts look to be positive for the barley demand outlook going forward.
  • Domestic markets in the UK remain quiet, although notionally there is a big carry forward from harvest as the market tries to incentivise growers to keep barley later into the season.

Rapeseed

  • US weather is still forecast hot into the weekend, but then shows isolated showers in most places and cooler temperatures for the next few weeks. As such, some weather premium is starting to come out of current prices. Funds continue to reduce market longs in beans and oil.
  • Soybean ratings fell 1% this week from 62% good/excellent to 61%.
  • In South America, consultant Safras estimates the 2022/23 soybean crop could reach 151.5 mln t, up 20%.
  • China’s lockdowns continue to plague the country’s economy. Just shy of 20% of the population remain confined to their houses. Manufacturing is affected and product demand is still poor. In addition China’s farmers are experiencing extreme heat and heavy rainfall, which seems to be worsening year on year. After nearly two months out of the US market, USDA confirmed a new crop soybean sale of 136,000t yesterday.
  • Energy markets rallied again before closing sharply lower in the last two sessions. Russia re-opened the Nord Stream pipeline on Thursday following routine downtime.
  • Malaysian palm oil prices closed down 1.95% (-77 ringgit). Indonesia have decided to cut export taxes and are rumoured to have delayed the increase in bio-fuel mandate to 35%.
  • Canadian canola futures closed lower this week after heavy rain spread across Alberta and Saskatchewan over the past few days, which will help crops. Canada’s agriculture ministry reported that most crops in Saskatchewan are in fair to good condition.
  • Matif rapeseed traded lower again, back close to levels seen before Russia’s invasion of Ukraine. Better EU rapeseed yields added to ample seed supply on the nearby positions, pushing prices lower. Low water levels on the Rhine are causing logistical delays, but prices are now a long way from the season’s highs, which is not enticing any farmer selling.

Oats

  • Oat markets remain pretty lack lustre, with minimal firm bids in the market place to really get things going.
  • Global weather has been mixed, with some areas suffering from adverse weather conditions over the course of the last week. Europe has suffered from abnormally high temperatures and this may have damaged yields on some of the spring oat crops across large areas of western Europe.
  • Canada continues to be the key driver for the global oat market, and despite more favourable weather in the forecast, there remains some concerns as to whether the country will be able to achieve the bounce in production that is currently forecast (3.88mln t vs 2.6 mln t last year and the 3.71 mln t five-year average, according to Oat information July’22). That said, it is currently expected that Canada will have sufficient supplies to satisfy its typical requirements.
  • Here in the UK, early cut winter crops have shown promise, with anecdotal yields being 10-15% higher than average and quality ranging from 57-63kg/hl, compared with the usual 50-52kg/hl. However, with the majority of the winter crop expected to be cut in the next 10 days, some caution should be taken regarding these results. Furthermore, spring oats have certainly suffered in areas where the crop has been progressing through grain-fill, therefore we are currently anticipating a variable oat harvest and working off an overall average yield at present.
  • Bottom line, EU buyers are still awaiting further confirmation of milling oat quality before coming into the market, therefore it’s all still to play for.

Pulses

Beans

  • The first new crop winter beans were harvested in Cambridgeshire this week and yielded 4.2t/ha, but were only feed quality.
  • Recent hot weather has brought on the harvesting date and the bulk of the crop will be harvested earlier than usual, in line with the likes of peas.
  • Crop conditions in the Baltic are good, with much cooler temperatures experienced recently compared to Western Europe, and reasonable levels of rainfall.
  • It is a little dry in Australia and crop production estimates have been reduced in recent weeks. However, crop production is still expected to be very high and Australia is likely to again take the biggest market share into North Africa this year.

Peas

  • Given the recent warm & dry conditions, peas will begin to harvest a few weeks earlier this season. A handful of growers in Lincolnshire & East Anglia have begun cutting their new crop peas this week. We expect the numbers to increase prior and into the weekend.
  • We have heard mixed reports on yields, but at this early stage it is not much to go on. Given the drought resistance from marrowfat peas, we would like to see some of the yields holding well and providing good quality. From the few samples we have seen bleaching has been low and the peas look a good green colour.
  • Competitive marrowfat buybacks are being received well for 2023/24 season. With the September-October movement period now closed, please ensure you book onto the November/December movements to avoid disappointment. Bonuses for better quality are available and a grower loyalty scheme can also be utilised if you have grown marrowfats for us this year.

Seed

  • From the seed crops harvested so far, yields and quality are good. With seed production starting imminently, and key varieties beginning to look very tight, we would advise placing orders as soon as possible to avoid disappointment.
  • If you are worried about ordering rapeseed and not having the right conditions to drill, consider DUPLO; available on a sale or return deal allowing you to return up to 75% of your seed.
  • Now is the time to be ordering companion crops. We have some great options to choose from to help aid establishment of the OSR. Contact your farm trader for more information.
  • There is still a range of environmental stewardship, grass and cover crop mixes available.
  • Please enquire with farm traders regarding stubble turnips as we move closer to August.

Fertiliser

  • India’s tender for 500,000t ended this week with offers totalling 1.76 mln t. Prices were lower than initial expectations and lower than previous trades concluded from the Middle East.
  • UK granular urea prices remain flat, balanced by an evidentially weaker global urea market and current high European AN prices. Urea remains highly competitive on a £/kg N basis against the AN product.
  • Nord Stream 1 is reportedly back in operation today, but Europe continues to remain on the edge of a complete energy crisis if Russian gas becomes unavailable entirely.
  • European and UK forward gas prices remain near all-time highs, which will ultimately create a price floor in the European AN market and nitrogen markets.
  • Phosphates continue to trade at their highest levels. Chinese export restrictions will keep these prices elevated for the foreseeable.
  • Potash prices are easing on global markets with demand destruction being seen in South American markets. It could be after autumn before corrections to be seen in UK potash markets.
