This week: Head of grain trading, Jonathan Lane, and national wheat trader, Freddie Humfrey, give an update on another very interesting week in the markets with weather and politics continuing to be the focus here.

Wheat

  • Weather and politics continue to be the focus of the grains markets.
  • An improving weather situation in Canada and the US spring wheat states has boosted North American wheat output and, with the Russian crop being talked higher again and Australia’s weather looking beneficial for wheat output, the global wheat S&D is now looking pretty comfortable.
  • On the other hand, the global maize market continues to be very tight. The EU looks like it will have the lowest crop in 15 years and demand has to be rationed and/or strong export volumes have to come out of Ukraine to solve the balance sheet.
  • Russia has been sounding off with accusations that the West is not honouring a commitment to help Russia’s agricultural exports reach global markets, which Moscow saw as part of the deal to lift the blockade on Ukraine’s grain exports. Russia hinted that the grain export corridor set up to facilitate those shipments may be under threat if European governments don’t start playing ball.
  • This prompted a sharp rally in the markets yesterday as the global maize balance really needs Ukraine export volumes and the wheat balance sheet cannot really afford a significant volume of demand moving from maize to wheat.
  • The UK market continues to be dominated by currency moves and our ongoing question is what are we going to do with our UK wheat surplus? After a further upward revision to our UK crop size to 15.8 mln t, we are now pegging the UK exportable surplus at 2.1 mln t.
  • However, the UK doesn’t seem to be making much headway with this figure. The struggle to generate any significant volume of export demand continues, despite the sharp drop in sterling. There are the odd cargoes creeping through and some underlying demand for UKs and feed wheat, but Russian wheat is not helping attempts to land any North African business.
  • In summary, the overall situation has not changed a lot this week. On a macro level, the increased availability from the Black Sea now means there is probably enough wheat in the world. However, market direction will crucially depend on export pace out of Russia and Ukraine.
  • Ukraine’s export capacity has the potential to ship a much larger volume than we are working with, but the shipping corridor has to remain in place and domestic internal logistics have to improve if we are going to see a jump in these volumes.

Malting Barley

  • Malting barley prices are firmer on the week, as the recent sell-off in flat price and premiums grinds to a halt. With a firming feed base and lower cash malting values, farmers aren’t engaging in the market at current malting premiums.
  • The uplift in futures is helping to strike a friendly tone in the market, and sellers are relaxed for the moment. We see buying interest creeping back into FOB and domestic markets against little selling interest.
  • Helped by weaker sterling, the UK domestic market has narrowed the gap vs FOB markets, which helps to keep a bid underlying in the UK.

Feed Barley

  • Feed barley markets are firmer on the week as futures markets make their way back up to recent highs. Barley remains competitive in feed rations, and as a result, has managed to keep pace with the rally in wheat.
  • Domestic demand has picked up slightly, as consumers take cover with prices moving higher, and we are starting to see signs of more spot demand in the market. We are also starting to see faster offtakes, indicating that demand is picking up.
  • Export markets have been rather quiet again this week, and the UK is struggling to connect in destination markets, although on a FOB basis coasters out of the UK do look competitive.
  • Farmer selling is still ticking along slowly, which is to be expected at this time of year. The market will be decided in the short term by whether we see supply or demand come to the table first.

Rapeseed

  • There are lots of factors affecting outside market movement at the moment, with most countries fighting record inflation levels, higher energy costs, and continued lockdowns in China, which is ultimately making investors very nervous.
  • After a long weekend in the United States, markets reopened lower on Tuesday and have continued to drift since.
  • US weather now has more rain in the forecast for the east but the plains remain dry. Overall it looks more favourable than it did a few months ago.
  • Soybean crop ratings were left unchanged this week at 57% good/excellent. Stone X increased its US yield forecast to 51.8 bpa vs 51.3 bpa previously.
  • South American crop prospects continue to grow, which also weighs on prices. This week Informa increased their 2022 soybean crop to 151 mln t (+1.5 mln t) vs the USDA at 149 mln t. Farmer selling in Argentina has picked up slightly following the introduction of a new “Soybean Peso”, which gives a more favourable rate to the farmer. This will allow more exports, which in turn will generate more export taxes for the government.
  • This comes at a time when the world’s biggest consumer, China, still battles with its zero Covid policy. There are ultimately still concerns over their economic growth. Crude oil imports for August were reported to be 9% lower vs last year, and they also only took significantly less soybeans and veg oil in August vs last year.
  • Oil markets fell again, with crude oil prices now falling back over 5% from the start of the week. Malaysian palm prices closed lower following reports of higher stocks.
  • In Australia, ABARES increased crop prospects of canola to 6.6 mln t vs the trade at 7 mln t.
  • In Canada, prices closed lower this week with harvest now having started in some regions. Stocks basis the end of July were reported sharply below trade estimates, actually a nine-year low, but that seems to give the market limited support.
  • MATIF rapeseed traded close to recent lows this week, following the wider complex. We now remain in a downtrend and given we have a more comfortable balance sheet this season, we need a new supply issue or increase in product demand to push prices higher.

Oats

  • Global grain markets continue to suffer from wild volatility driven largely by the geopolitical situation in the Black Sea with a backdrop of global recession. Consumers are nervous about the demand destruction caused by high levels of inflation, therefore it is currently uncertain as to how this will affect the buying appetite of consumers for oats.
  • The results of the Scandinavian harvest has been the main stumbling block affecting trade of milling oats into western Europe. However, with reports of a wide availability of good quality milling oats, we have seen sellers become more aggressive. The differential between milling oats and other feed grains is widening with this fresh supply pressure and if futures continue to rally we could see this widen further.
  • Here in the UK, farmers are now firmly focused on land work and preparations for next year’s crop. Therefore, with harvest a distant memory, the selling pressure has subsided. Milling oat prices could see a rise in the coming weeks if demand coincides with a lack of sellers. However, any price rally will likely be tempered by the large surplus of milling oat quality.
  • Bottom line, there is a surplus of milling oats in the UK and we will need to price ourselves to be competitive for exports.

Pulses

Beans

  • The bean harvest is approaching completion. Unfortunately yield & quality doesn’t seem to be improving as we move further north.
  • High levels of insect damage still seen across the crop, with spring beans, in particular, being smaller in size compared with previous years.
  • The market is following wheat futures on a one-for-one basis and is, therefore, firmer on the week.
  • The Baltic harvest has commenced. Yields are significantly better than last year and quality appears fairly good. Currently, it is offered at a discount to UK-origin beans into Egypt.
  • Australian crop production forecasts still projects a large crop of over 800,000mt, which should keep a lid on the human consumption market for the next couple of months. Therefore we do not expect a rally in UK human consumption premiums anytime soon.

Peas

  • UK pea harvest is almost at completion, totaling around 95% nationally, with the late drillers being the only ones with fields left to cut.
  • Yields posted at just below 3t/ha, which is down year on year, but slightly improved from earlier indications. Quality is varied, but good green colour seems prominent in both marrowfats and large blue peas, which would benefit those on our no-bleaching claims contract with bonuses for higher bleach. Bleaching average is at 9.5%.
  • Insect damage at an average of 1.9% did raise cause for concern, however, with the new needle cylinders at our Long Sutton facility, the issue is being mitigated well by the machines and reducing it down to within our customer’s specifications.
  • We are keen buyers of open-market marrowfats and our 2023 buybacks are all still available. Please contact your farm trader.

Seed

  • Feed wheat varieties SY Insitor and SY Gleam have both once again had a great year in trials. Gleam is continuing to provide consistently high yields and remains a popular choice on farm. Insitor offers huge root biomass, which has been an advantage in the dry conditions this season. Seed availability is currently good for both varieties, but is selling fast.
  • For any last-minute cropping decisions, we have good availability of extremely vigorous hybrid OSR Duplo from DSV. Duplo is suited for the later drilling spot and is supported by all the usual traits, including RLM7 resistance to stem canker, TuYV, and Podshatter.
  • We have a range of countryside stewardship mixes available, suitable for various scheme requirements.
  • There are still some great options for cover crop mixes including Grabber, Sprinter, and Winter Revivor as well as cover crop straights.
  • For more information on our autumn portfolio please view our seed varieties information guide here.

Fertiliser

  • Granular urea remains stable after rising over £100/t in around 14 days. It remains the most cost-effective source of nitrogen in the UK.
  • European production of ammonia and finished nitrogen fertiliser products remains severely curtailed.
  • Gas prices have eased off most recent highs, however, the production/supply of nitrate and urea from the region has already been impacted.
  • UK AN manufacturer CF remains out of the market for new sales, highlighting the current difficulties in the economics of producing nitrogen fertilisers in the UK and Europe at present.
  • DAP has seen a small correction in pricing, while potash and phosphate remain stubbornly stable at present.
