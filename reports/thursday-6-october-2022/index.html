Thursday 6 October 2022

Subscribe to the ADM Agriculture YouTube channel for the latest grain, seed & fertiliser market updates, and much more!

Wheat

  • The US market has remained virtually unchanged week on week as inflationary demand concerns offset a bullish USDA US wheat outlook, and the longevity of the Ukrainian grain export corridor.
  • USDA trimmed US 2022 wheat production by 3.5 mln t in its latest report, reflecting declines in both harvested area and yield.
  • After a slow start, Russian wheat exports are now seen at a record 43.4 mln t according to SovEcon, citing a growing harvest and rising import demand as the rationale for the upgrade.
  • Despite this, Russia may start providing trade finance to importers as an additional means to offset sanctions which continue to slow the pace of exports.
  • As expected, Ukraine’s grain exports so far this marketing year have fallen to about 8.6 mln t, down 40% year on year. Shipments so far include 4.8 mln t of corn, 3 mln t of wheat and 750,000t of barley.
  • With an expected 70 mln t harvest, Ukrainian storage capacity is again raising concerns with the government ‘hoping for a prolongation of the mandate to bring grain out of Ukrainian ports’.
  • Ukrainian farmers had planted 1.2 mln t of winter grains as of 3 October, including 1.1 mln ha of wheat which is 27% of the total planned area.
  • Rains in October would sustain the revised Argentine 2022 wheat crop estimate of 17.5 mln t released by the Buenos Aires Grain Exchange, although continued dryness would place both area and yields at risk.
  • Iraq plans to increase wheat plantings to about 1 mln ha for 2023/24 season, following the driest year since 1930, which dramatically lowered the country’s harvested area this year.
  • Intervention by the Bank of England and the government’s U-turn on proposed tax cuts has seen sterling recover from its lows following the mini-budget, leading to a £6.25/t week on week decline in London wheat futures.

Malting Barley

  • Malting barley markets have continued to climb over the last week, primarily on lack of origination. With steady feed barley markets, ex-farm quality premiums are also higher, now over £40/t at their highest.
  • FOB markets have quietened as the rebound in sterling kills demand in the short term. However, we still see demand for UK malting barley underlying, particularly with our favourable quality vs French and Scandinavian origins, and good demand in the forecast from Spain.
  • In a very thin market, the question for prices in the short term will be whether supply or demand engages first. However, longer term, both the UK and the wider continent has a surplus of malting barley so the S&D looks bearish. As with all things at the moment, the developing events in the Black Sea will no doubt keep the market on its toes.

Feed Barley

  • The UK feed barley market remains stuck in a rut, and activity is once again limited. Anecdotally, domestic consumers are exposed in the forward positions, however the market still remains paralysed by the ever-present geopolitical uncertainty.
  • Nearby European export markets are similarly slow, and buyers are not engaging other than to secure spot shipments where required.
  • International markets have seen a little more interest this week, with Turkey’s TMO announcing a tender for 500,000t and Tunisia tendering for 100,000t. It will be interesting to see the results of the Turkey tender in particular, as the position falls across Oct/Nov shipments and therefore beyond the end date for the current Black Sea grain corridor deal. If supply from the Black Sea is halted, buyers will potentially have to look into northern Europe to secure supplies.

Rapeseed

  • It has been another big week for outside markets. The US dollar closed at a two-week low after one of its biggest falls of the year, before recovering shortly after.
  • US weather remains favourable, with most locations staying dry for the next few weeks. Soybean harvest progress was reported at 22% complete vs 31% last year (five-year average at 25%) and is expected to be closer to 50% next week.
  • Soybeans struggled to hold on to their higher prices. The dollar rallied again, which added pressure. River levels are also said to be getting very low, which will cause a slowdown in exports. USDA announced a sale of 110,000t of US soybeans to an unknown destination (not thought to be China).
  • China is still on holiday this week, but there are reports that the country’s economy is worsening rather than recovering. Real estate companies are in financial difficulty, which is forcing banks to buy bonds.
  • Energy markets were firmer this week following Opec’s decision to increase production by 2 mln barrels. West Texas Intermediate oil is trading at $88 – the recent lift lent support to veg oils. In addition to higher energy prices, India was reported to have increased palm oil imports by 200,000t in September to 1.2 mln, vs. 1 mln t in August. Reuters reported palm oil stocks for September at 2.265 mln t (+8% from August). The Malaysian Palm Oil Board will release its stocks report on 11 Oct.
  • In South America, showers persist across the north-east of Argentina, but the rest of the country is dry. Brazil still has rain in the south of the country, slowing planting progress to 3.8% complete vs 4.1% complete last year.
  • In Australia, harvest advances, but wet weather in the east will hinder progress. The impact on crop conditions remains to be seen.
  • Canadian canola futures closed higher this week. Firmer energy markets lent support to the complex. Harvest pressure seems to be easing as many regions start to finish. Alberta and Saskatchewan are now very close to completion.
  • MATIF rapeseed traded back to levels not seen since August before succumbing to the fall in the US dollar we saw on Tuesday. Higher energy prices and firmer veg oil prices lent support to the market.
  • Here in the UK, rapeseed prices dip from recent highs. Sterling firmed back to trade over 1.1500, which also pressured prices.

Oats

  • Oat markets in Europe have fallen in some areas and risen in others this week. Lower prices in the wider grains market following concerns over recession have edged feed prices lower. However, this is capped by the fact oat prices failed to follow the wheat-led rally triggered since last Friday’s USDA report. Bids for milling oats into Germany and Holland remain few and far between, with processors awaiting the consumption demand from the retail sector before being in a position to offer firm bids.
  • Here in the UK, oat prices have rallied slightly, with consumers paying higher prices in order to tempt sellers. Sterling’s recovery following the UK governments U-turn on tax has made UK prices less competitive than they were last week. However, UK milling oats are among the cheapest on the market, which should see further export sales in the coming weeks if the demand is there.
  • Bottom line, a lack of buyers continues to result in a lack of market direction. We could however see things improve now that we are in October.

Pulses

Beans

  • From a human consumption perspective, demand remains thin, as per the last couple of weeks. Baltic beans are providing a cheaper origin to many consumers for quarter four of 2022, and with the large Australian crop coming next year, it could continue to quell human consumption demand.
  • Feed wise, with sterling rallying slightly, we saw the value of some imported proteins reduce on the week, meaning the spread between beans and those imported proteins has thinned. We believe beans will be well supported from this point onwards as there is still reasonable value there as a protein source.

Peas

  • Feed pea demand seems non-existent in the spot positions, both domestically and globally. We do see this picking up in the final month of the year and heading into early 2023. Consumers are comfortable with their positions.
  • Human consumption pea demand remains strong. With marrowfats still in high demand, we are keen to increase the amount of buybacks we have on the system, where there is still space for pre-Christmas movement in 2023. Book now to avoid disappointment.
  • Yellow and large blue peas also host demand in the final quarter of the year. Most consumers are covered on large blues, but will look to fill in their gaps over the next month or so.
  • Our Long Sutton facility is almost finished with its first run of yellows this season, which has been a great success. We are likely to release yellows terms over the coming weeks.
  • The next VIPea event is to take place on the 15 November 2022. We are keen to have as many growers as we can on-site with optional site and laboratory tours available. Please RSVP your invitation prior to 1 November to secure your place!

Seed

  • We have a wide range of winter wheat seed floor stocks available for immediate delivery, including high yielding Champion from DSV and KWS Extase. With solid disease resistance and opportunity for milling premiums there is no wonder KWS Extase had the largest C2 winter wheat seed market last year. Get in touch with your farm trader for more information.
  • Winter barley seed is largely sold out, with the exception of small quantities of hybrid SY Baracooda and conventional six-row KWS Feeris.
  • View our latest seed YouTube video here, covering the importance of seed treatments with Tim Eaton, seed product manager at Certis Belchim.

Fertiliser

  • Global urea trade has been relatively lacklustre following the Ethiopian tender for circa 950,000t.
  • Speculation on when India will return to tender continues to grow. Egyptian urea has traded $40/t higher over this week.
  • Currency has been a major factor in UK pricing over the last two weeks. The pound/dollar exchange has reversed from low of 1.0539 to highs yesterday at 1.1495.
  • Urea prices have seen some stability in the week and we therefore have Jan-Mar delivery available.
  • UAN prices have remained stable and autumn and spring deliveries are available for all nitrogen and nitrogen sulphur grades.
  • Liquid inhibitor, AdvaNShield, is a cost-effective option to reduce nitrogen losses to the environment. AdvaNShield can be added to existing orders that are not delivered as well as new orders.
  • Phosphate and Potash prices are yet to see any changes on UK pricing and have been flat for over a month now.
  • Mosaic, a major North American phosphate manufacturer, confirmed damage from Storm Ian. This delayed delivery execution and North American pricing increased slightly on the news.
