Thursday 23 June 2022

Subscribe to the ADM Agriculture YouTube channel for the latest grain, seed & fertiliser market updates and much more!

Wheat

  • Another choppy week on the markets as the ever-present topics of weather and geopolitics play out, with all benchmark wheat futures losing ground.
  • The rapid pace of the US winter wheat harvest has helped ease markets on a fundamental level. About 25% of the area has been cut, around 10% ahead of last year’s pace, as the dry weather allows the southern states to make cracking progress.
  • CBOT wheat has opened sharply down on overnight trade for two consecutive sessions now, as the wheat market steadily starts to feel heavier. Week on week, the main wheat markets have moved as follows:
    • Dec22 CBOT wheat is down $28.49/t
    • Dec22 MATIF wheat is down €23.75/t
    • Nov22 ICE London wheat is down £17.30/t
  • Reports that the Russian wheat harvest is underway are adding further pressure, along with SovEcon raising its production estimates again, by 1 mln t to 89.6mmt. This additional tonnage will go straight on to the country’s exportable surplus, pushing potential shipments towards 43 mln t, with some analysts calling for a number closer to 45 mln t.
  • Talks continue between Russia and Turkey about setting up a humanitarian export corridor to move grain out of Ukrainian ports. However, reports of steadily increasing levels of infrastructure damage are raising questions about the feasibility of these corridors, even if all the other hurdles can be passed. Yesterday, both Bunge and Viterra’s terminals in Mykolaiv were hit in the latest round of Russian attacks on the port city, with both companies confirming there had been damage.
  • The French wheat harvest has begun, with early reports suggesting bushel weights in the region of 74kg/hl. However, moistures are reportedly low and we have heard of silo intakes in the south and west in excess of 78kg/hl. The Paris Basin area is expected to perform even better once harvest arrives there in the coming days.
  • There is some concern around forecasted rain across some of the areas that are currently cutting and the associated impact to quality – the rain will likely stop play for a couple of days.
  • The UK old crop market is now essentially done. Consumers are again notable by their absence, whilst the last remaining loads of old crop come to market in a desperate bid to find an outlet as the industry empty sheds ahead of harvest.
  • The weakness playing out in the new crop market sees the Nov22 ICE London feed wheat contract heading towards the key technical level of £283/t. If this level cannot hold, we could well see values continue to decline, with the next stop around £270/t.

Malting Barley

  • France’s winter barley harvest is well underway.
  • Yields are average to good. Quality is mixed.
  • It’s too early to say what effect the recent extreme heat has had on the French spring barley crop.
  • Scandinavia growing conditions remain favourable and a good crop is expected.
  • There is talk of an early winter barley harvest in England, perhaps as early as the end of next week.
  • Malting barley prices have dropped this week but are still at historical highs.

Rapeseed

  • CBOT soybeans are trading sharply lower this week. US farmers are reported to be 94% planted now on soybeans, with an estimated 5.45 mln acres to go. Crop ratings have fallen two points from 70% good/excellent to 68%.
  • At the end of the month the USDA will estimate the US soybean area as of 1 June. Some forecasts are calling for the area to be up 1 mln acres on USDA’s March figure.
  • The US weather story is now looking less concerning. The next 5-6 day weather forecasts shows cooler temperatures and scattered showers, which erodes the recently built-in weather premium. However, longer term forecasts show it getting hot/dry again.
  • In China, the effects of COVID-19 are still impacting the economy Lockdowns continue. Another major property developer is being held in default, and now a heat wave is not only causing issues for crop production, but with energy consumption so high, the government is increasing coal production to try and stop widespread power-cuts.
  • Energy markets declined. Oil prices traded a five-week low, but bounced from session lows. The bearish situation in China, added to higher exports out of Iran over the past few weeks, has weighed on prices.
  • Veg oils just followed everything else for the ride. Palm oil tried to bounce, but then closed 470 ringgit (9.44%) lower again yesterday. Bean oil closed sharply lower and rapeseed followed. Supplies seem to be outweighing demand and following energy markets lower.
  • Canadian canola prices closed over $50 down on the July yesterday. Matif rapeseed traded down to levels not seen since the start of April. EU harvest has started and seed availability looks comfortable for now.
  • Demand for products is slipping and there is nothing new to support prices whilst everything else in the complex trades lower. EU harvest has started and the threat of biofuel mandate changes is pressuring prices.
  • Sterling fell again this morning to trade below 1.16.

Oats

  • European buyers of both feed and milling are all focussing on the new crop, which is fast approaching as a reason to try and bid markets lower.
  • Consumers are expecting some form of harvest pressure, but farmer selling is virtually non-existent as growers await a greater understanding of harvest quality. Anecdotally, the crop in Spain has suffered significantly from unseasonably high temperatures, with production estimates 15-30% lower than expected. Quality is also reportedly poor and this should see a greater import requirement than last year.
  • Here in the UK, markets are very quiet, with millers awaiting results of the new crop quality along with buying demand from their customers before coming into the market. Feed compounders have been active and continue to buy feed oats, providing they are a big enough discount to barley.
  • Bottom line, it is still very much all to play for, and all eyes remain on the weather.

Pulses

Beans

  • Old crop export demand remains thin and, with most domestic consumers covered, this has led to quiet farmer selling week on week. We have seen a pullback in prices following the wheat market volatility, although historically this is the time of year we tend to see some drop offs in markets.
  • New crop feed beans demand also tight, with farmers holding their cards close to their chest and keeping a keen eye on the wheat market, which is falling lower again leading to the flat price for new crop beans dropping.
  • Crop conditions look good across the UK. However, the dry spell over the past two weeks leaves them needing rain, which according to the forecast may arrive at the back end of next week.

Peas

  • Old crop peas are effectively complete, with domestic buyers covered for the season. Some opportunities will arise during June and potentially in August, but it will very much so be spot demand.
  • New crop peas are looking fantastic in the fields and the expected rain over the next week will help crops further.
  • Our marrowfat buybacks are available for the 2023/24 season. Please contact your farm trader if you are interested. With fertiliser prices remaining high, the (average) 50kg/ha of residual nitrogen would really benefit your following wheat crops and help with costs.
  • Thank you to all of growers who attended our inaugural VIPea event. We plan to conduct another one later this year, so please keep an eye out for the ‘save the date’.

Seed

  • We have a great OSR portfolio. If you are worried about establishment, DK Excited is available on a market-leading establishment scheme, allowing up to £100 per pack rebate for any crop that fails to establish.
  • Companion cropping is a great option for helping aid the establishment of OSR. The amount of area drilled with companion crops is continuing to grow.
  • We have a great range of countryside stewardship mixes available to suit many schemes.
  • We also offer a variety of grass seeds to suit many requirements, whether that be intensive cutting, grazing, hay production and more.

Fertiliser

  • Global granular urea markets have seen an uptick in activity this week. Reported trades for the 46N product have risen about $55/t since Monday.
  • Volatility in European gas markets continues to impact ammonia production in the region, with two plants shuttering operations since last week.
  • European nitrate pricing and availability continues to create a premium destination market for other nitrogen products.
  • Spot UK ammonium nitrate pricing and forward granular urea pricing remain unchanged at present, holding firm despite seasonal lulls in the market.
  • Liquid urea ammonium nitrate pricing is available for spot delivery and all nitrogen and nitrogen sulphur grades remain available.
  • Phosphate and potash prices continue to remain stable-to-firm despite the demand destruction being seen in South American markets.
