Thursday 24 November 2022

Wheat

The US market is down $9/t on the week as reports of EU wheat trading into the US, along with the extension of the Ukrainian export corridor, highlights the current lack of competitiveness of US wheat supplies onto the global market.

EU prices are virtually unchanged, with confirmation of Chinese purchases and EU wheat trading into the US underpinning the market, with traders expecting exports to accelerate in the new year.

UK prices are down about £3.50/t as a firmer pound, sluggish UK demand and more aggressive Black Sea offers weigh on farm gate prices.

Despite no confirmation from Russia, and with no sides amending any of the terms, the export corridor agreement was automatically extended for a further 120 days last weekend.

The European Commission, partner financial institutions and the World Bank are to mobilise €1bn by the end of 2023 to boost the capacity of solidarity lanes, essential corridors to help Ukraine export agricultural commodities via neighbouring EU states.

World grain stockpiles in the 2022/23 season are now seen at an eight-year low of 580mln t, according to the International Grains Council, with global maize stocks at their lowest level for a decade.

Argentina’s agriculture ministry has put the 2022/23 wheat crop at 13.4mln t, down almost 40% on the year, but still much higher than Buenos Aires and Rosario grain exchanges.

Ukraine’s grain exports reached 15.6mln t as of 16 November, down 31% on the year. The total includes 6mln t of wheat, 1.3mln of barley and 8.3mln t of maize.

Russian market analyst IKAR has boosted the country’s wheat export forecast to 44mln t, from 42mln t previously, whilst raising its production estimate by 0.5mln t to a record 101.5mln t.

China is buying more Australian wheat than ever this season, even though diplomatic relations between the two countries have frayed in recent years.

Following the setting up of Chinese import protocols for Brazilian maize, the first ship is due to leave Brazil this week. Trade talk suggests that the country’s 2023 maize exports could reach 50mln t, bolstered by Chinese demand.

Farmers in the world’s second biggest wheat growing country, India, had planted 10.15mln ha of the crop as of 8 November, an increase of almost 15% on the year.

Egypt’s state grain buyer (GASC) is believed to have purchased at least 3.55mln t of wheat so far this season, in tenders and private talks with suppliers, compared with about 2.95mln t a year ago.

Strategy Grains reported that EU wheat stocks should end the season at a ‘very comfortable level’ with largest inventories expected to be in the eastern countries due to reduced exports.

AHDB reported that the UK is likely to plant 1.8mln t ha of wheat in the 2023 season, down just 0.1% from the current year, although high fertiliser prices could hamper the quality, and quantity, if farmers curb usage.

 

Malting Barley

Malting barley markets across the continent are feeling pressure as demand remains elusive, both in domestic and FOB markets.

Sterling strength is also helping to add pressure in the UK.

Premiums to feed barley still look good value to the farmer as feed prices fall.

New crop values remain supported on a lack of farmer selling and demand moving from South America to Northern Europe as the Argentinian crop continues to deteriorate. Ex-farm premiums for new crop look very good as feed barley markets feel pressure.

 

Feed Barley

The slow demand story continues for feed barley markets.

The extension of the Black Sea grain corridor for a further 120 days is not helping to stimulate buyers in Spain, as cheap wheat and corn to the Spanmed coast undercuts barley. Meanwhile stocks in EU destinations are high, helping to keep buyers relaxed.

Domestic buyers in the UK are similarly inactive. Most have comfortable coverage into January and the market remains cautious about demand destruction going later into the New Year.

Prices remain supported relative to wheat particularly in the deferred positions, despite low demand, as farmer selling is equally slow.

Rapeseed

US soybeans closed in the green as traders position themselves ahead of Thanksgiving. The recent fall in U.S dollar lent support; markets will reopen Friday afternoon where traders will look to export sales.

Showers are forecast for the south of Argentina next week, but it remains dry elsewhere. Argentina still has some catching up to do, with plantings only 20% complete versus 35% on average.

Weather in Brazil is unchanged but nearby logistics around Mato Grosso are being slowed due to blockades. There are no weather concerns to report. With light showers for the next few weeks, the trade now looks to see if Brazil will be on track for a 150mln t crop. Brazilian plantings are 80% complete, compared with 86% last year.

Despite a private exporter reporting a sale of 110,000mt of soybeans to China there have been no significant market activity to report. Covid cases in China again spiked in recent days, leading authorities to increase testing across a number of cities. President Xi is still committed to a zero covid policy, which may lead to further lockdowns that may have additional economic impact.

Energy prices fell sharply again with crude oil down $4 from recent highs. US demand for gas and diesel is weaker than expected and the EU has announced a price cap on Russian oil. Whilst this pressured veg oils, they found support from the potential for more veg oils to be used in the biofuel ration.

Canadian canola prices fell for the sixth session a row and found little support from the rally in soybeans.

Matif rapeseed also struggled to find any support in the last week and closed sharply lower Wednesday night. Prices are now trading below €600, which was viewed as a nearby support level. Supplies seem ample in the nearby positions and, without a new story to trade on, prices could drift from here.

UK prices felt the impact of the falling Matif, while sterling rallied to trade over 1.1600 yesterday, which will also pressure UK prices.

Oats

The European oat market has followed the wider grain markets lower this week with feed oats trading at approximately €5 down. Bids are also falling given the weight of supplies looking for a home.

Milling oats are struggling to capture much buying interest and it would appear that the UK remains the cheapest supplier into West Europe.

Here in the UK, milling oat buying activity remains relaxed with the fall in wheat prices contributing to a desire to bid prices lower. However, given the estimated reduction in planned area of 10% for crop 2023/24, the UK needs to be mindful of ensuring we have enough supplies for next year.

Exports into Europe from the UK stand at 73,500t as of the 20th November. It is possible that we will reach 120,000t again this marketing season. If realised this will make the balance sheet less over-supplied than it may currently seem.

Bottom line, demand for oats into Europe remains slow but the pace of exports out of the UK is very strong.

Pulses

Faba beans continue to drift lower on the back of very limited demand, either domestic or export.

At current prices beans look competitive against other mid-proteins but consumer demand is limited. Many have covered their requirements for the winter position.

Human consumption buyers are still side-lined. Inflation is rampant in Egypt, so consumers are simply eating less. With stocks high and a large Australian crop on the way, new buying interest for UK origin beans is likely to remain limited for the rest of the season.

There remains strong demand for old crop yellow and marrowfat peas, so prices are holding up well. However, the large blue pea market appears over-supplied, and prices continue to drift lower.

Fertiliser

Urea pricing in the UK has softened this week thanks to the stronger pound, which improved on the back of economic data in the UK this week and a change in future FED rate hike expectations. But future UK economic data could be detrimental to sterling.

North America remains one of the lowest-priced urea markets in the world. Expectations remain that prices could pick up prior to spring, with potential logistical complications looming with low river levels and rail strikes on the horizon.

Price rises in North America could lift North African and Europe levels. Urea still trades at a discount to ammonium nitrate in Europe and has room for a £50-85/t increase when considering comparative nitrogen unit costs.

Higher winter rainfall this year, in comparison to last winter, could reduce Soil Nitrogen Supply (SNS) levels in the UK. This would increase demand for nitrogen in the spring for those targeting higher yields and quality.

Farmers should ensure SNS levels are tested after winter drainage and ensure pH levels are at their optimum to get the most out of their nitrogen fertilisers.

MOP prices have levelled off. TSP and DAP prices have remained under some pressure. Interest in the products have increased following price corrections, but sales remain down historically, indicating more buying could occur which would support prices.
