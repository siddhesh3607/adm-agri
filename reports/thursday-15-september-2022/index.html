Thursday 15 September 2022

Her Majesty Queen Elizabeth II, who died on Thursday 8 September, took a great interest in the countryside and those who live and work there throughout her 70 years on the throne.

                                                                                                 Our sincere condolences go out to the Royal Family on their loss.

               Subscribe to the ADM Agriculture YouTube channel for the latest grain, seed & fertiliser market updates and more.

Wheat

  • It has been sombre week for the nation, which has witnessed both the shared grief of countries within the United Kingdom and beyond at the sad passing of Her Majesty Queen Elizabeth II as well as widespread thanksgiving for her 70 years of steadfast service and leadership.
  • This week saw the release of the September instalment of USDA’s global supply and demand estimates, which contained a fairly mundane report for grain, with numbers all falling within the range of trade expectations. Aside from this, it was another week of weather and politics.
  • The latest report from USDA was a tale of two crops, with the highlights below. In short, there has been partial demand switching from maize to wheat, although questions remain as to whether the US maize figure is too high.
  • On the wheat front there were few amendments of note, with the highlights being increases to both Russia’s production (+3mln t to 91mln t), which brought it more in line with private estimates, and Ukraine’s output (+1mln t to 20.5mln t). However, exports remained unchanged on both counts.
  • Turning to maize, various expected amendments were made, primarily sweeping global production cuts of 7mln t. Although partially offset by some loss of demand, these resulted in closing stocks falling 2.2mln t. In the US the yield was reduced 2.9bushels/acres to 172.5bu/ac, although this is still a way away from the ProFarmer tour figure of 168.1bu/ac. USDA reduced the harvested area by 1mln acres to 80.8mln ac.
  • Having been range bound for around nine weeks now, ICE London futures seem to have finally found some traction to the upside, pushing through the top of the range (c. £275/t), having closed above £277/t for the last two consecutive sessions. If the channel is confirmed as broken to the upside, this opens the door for a new range of £255 to £284, with a potential target of £295 after this.
  • Despite the weakness we are currently seeing in sterling and the widening of the spread between ICE London and MATIF wheat futures, the UK remains uncompetitive on global export markets where European feed premiums have pushed lower, offsetting the move in futures. Whilst there is a steady flow of primarily frame-based exports occurring at the moment to the likes of Ireland and Spain, there is little fresh demand, leaving around 1mln t of the exportable surplus unaccounted for. This would leave an exceptionally large carry-out that is potentially unworkable and would certainly sit on domestic old crop pricing relative to the new crop.
  • In summary, an overall similar picture to last week with few shocks to the market. At the macro level, Black Sea geopolitics and Northern Hemisphere maize concerns continue to be the main factors. More locally, the UK remains a reliable £4-5/t away from being export competitive, with ICE London futures needing to do the work relative to global markets to facilitate the shipping of what appears to be a sizeable exportable surplus.

Malting Barley

  • Malting barley markets are firmer on the week, as farmers turn away from selling malting barley at what are much lower premiums than just a few weeks ago.
  • Meanwhile, a flurry of export business has been concluded in recent weeks out of the UK, with continental buyers favouring UK quality over French/Danish origin.
  • This extra demand in combination with a lack of farmer selling and firmer feed markets is expected to support malting cash markets in the short term. However, the S&D in the UK and northern EU still looks comfortable which adds a bearish tone to the market going forwards.

Feed Barley

  • Feed barley markets are firmer on the week, following futures markets higher.
  • Barley still remains competitive in feed rations across the UK and Northern Europe. However, demand for another week is slow to come to the table, although we are starting to see this pick up.
  • Feed barley specific activity is limited, and once again prices continue to be dictated mainly by news headlines relating to the Russia-Ukraine war and global macro-economic factors.

Rapeseed

  • Weather in the US is now turning dry across most regions with any the rain staying over the lakes in the next few days before turning dry for the next two weeks.
  • On Monday USDA released its latest supply and demand update for September. Trade expectations prior to the report would lead most to think it would follow a similar trend to August thought they caught the trade off guard.  US soybean ending stocks for 2022/23 were reported below trade estimates at 200 mln bushels (average estimate of 246 mln bu).  In addition, they also reduced 2022/23 harvested soybean area to 86.6 mln ac (previously 87.3 mln ac) and lowered soyabean yields by 1.4 bu/ac to 50.5 bu/ac.  Chinese demand decreased by 1mln t to 97mln t. This left the market with a tighter than expected balance sheet which puts more pressure on a big South American crop to help fill the gap.
  • China is still in large-scale lockdown which poses concerns over economic growth. There were rumours that President Biden may impose sanctions on China to deter them from invading Taiwan whilst Chinese traders are continuing to favour South American soybeans over US origin.
  • In South American plantings have now started in parts of Brazil and Paraguay. Argentina will start in just over a month. Farmer selling in Argentina increased following recent sales to China. It was also noted that farmers want to sell beans now before the “Soy Peso” lapses at the end of the month.
  • Energy markets seem to be trading within a range for now. Whilst soy-oil and palm oil prices followed everything else higher after the USDA report, oil markets took back most of those gains yesterday, not helped by USDA increasing 2022/23 veg oil stocks by 370,000t. Stocks are now a record 30.41mmt.
  • Canadian canola prices dipped from the highs of the week but managed to find support following some harvest pressure last week. Yields will now be closely watched going forward.
  • Matif rapeseed is remains sharply off the highs, trading below €600/t in recent days. The EU rapeseed balance sheet looks ample, with exports flowing out of the Ukraine and Baltic regions. Matif oil demand hasn’t risen enough to outweigh the large supply and unless we see buying interest increase or a crop problem then prices could drift further.

Oats

  • The European oat market has started to show signs of increased buying activity over the course of the last week with both milling and feed buyers looking for offers. Feed oats seem to be of particular interest with offers from the Baltic states being most competitively priced. Poland who is typically an exporter of oats has been a buyer and this has added extra support to the feed market. Consequentially the differential between feed and milling has narrowed into western Europe, something that may continue given the good supply of EU milling oats.
  • Here in the UK farmer selling continues to be limited and as a result trade activity seems to be slow. Feed compounders are giving good underlying support to the market with feed and milling oats trading at the same level in some areas. The exceptional quality of our UK crop combined with weak £/€ is giving us the opportunity to compete against Scandinavian suppliers, therefore we may see a flow of milling oats out of the UK this season. The only question is how much.
  • Bottom line, there is a surplus of milling oats in the UK, and this has resulted in a drop in milling oat premiums. Therefore, we will need a rise in milling buying interest in order to push premiums higher.

Pulses

Peas

  • We are still awaiting some final yield reports from this year’s harvest, however early indications state a 3t/ha yield on marrowfats and 3.75t/ha on large blue peas. Bleaching this year across both commodities have performed well, at an average of 9.5% combined, meaning the majority of large blue peas met packing grade and marrowfats performed very well vs our contracts, where bonuses are applied for under 10% bleached.
  • Our buyback terms for next year’s marrowfat contracts are still available. With places running out on our November-December run, it is vital to get booked onto these to avoid disappointment and ensure movement in February 2024.
  • There is interest coming out of Southern Europe for feed peas and we are looking to supply a coaster during October. The same cannot be said for domestic buyers who are currently covered in the nearby positions, but we expect demand to increase as we head to Q4 and Q1 for next year.
  • As for human consumption, we have moved over 2000t of marrowfats into line 6 over the past six weeks, which has been a great start to the season. We now are going to change commodity to produce yellow peas. We are keen buyers on the open market so please get in touch with your farm trader if you have any open market yellows on farm.
  • Demand for green and yellow human consumption peas appears to be picking up, with the European market having the most interest. Marrowfats remain in short supply, but we believe our machinery has done an excellent job producing great quality.

Seed

  • Availability is now very limited across the trade for newly recommended feed wheat varieties KWS Dawsum and Champion.
  • Hybrid barley is now starting to sell out, however, we can still offer consistently high-yielding ADM exclusive SY Baracooda.
  • Get in touch with your farm trader now to hear what floor stocks we have available for quick delivery.
  • For any last-minute cropping decisions, we have good availability of extremely vigorous hybrid OSR Duplo from DSV. Duplo is suited for the later drilling spot and is supported by all the usual traits including RLM7 resistance to stem canker, TuYV, and Podshatter.
  • We have a range of grass seed mixes available to suit a range of requirements including cutting, grazing, and hay.
  • There is still time to get your countryside stewardship mixes, we have some great options that will abide by your scheme’s guidelines.

Fertiliser

  • India secured 874,000t from its most recent tender, below the 1mln t target. The country is expected to continue to negotiate the final tonnage.
  • Talks of an UN-backed ammonia pipeline opening through to Ukraine is one to watch for the European nitrate market.
  • EU and UK Natural gas caps for price and industry are something that will be closely watched also.
  • Natural gas prices still remain significantly higher than historical norms and confidence in maintaining European nitrate production is low given current dynamics.
  • Granular urea continues to be the most cost efficient and available source of nitrogen in the UK. Prices have fluctuated over the week but have settled slightly higher.
