Thursday 9 June 2022

This week: Jonathan Lane and Freddie Humfrey look at the current grain market here.

Wheat

  • Since our last report two weeks ago the US market has traded down $22/t, as reports of sluggish US exports, improving new crop prospects in some areas and talk of Ukrainian export corridors being set up weigh on prices.
  • EU prices have followed the weaker global trend, with new crop MATIF (Dec) and London (Nov) futures falling €21/t and £16/t respectively over the period.
  • Australia is forecast to produce another huge harvest this year, with wheat production estimated at the fourth highest level of 30.3 mln t, albeit well below last year’s record 36.3 mln t.
  • Russia’s new crop wheat harvest continues to be talked higher, with current estimates at between 85-87 mln t, which would place export potential at around 40 mln t.
  • Ukraine’s Grain Association has raised its forecast for this year’s wheat harvest to 19.2 mln t and corn to 26.1 mln t, placing export potential at 10 mln t and 15 mln t respectively.
  • Ukraine’s president said there could be as much as 75 mln t of grain stuck in the country by this autumn, with the agriculture ministry stating that failure to open up export corridors would lead to ‘catastrophic’ global price increases.
  • Kazakhstan is expected to harvest between 13.0-13.5 mln t of wheat this year, up 15%. However, the government plans to extend grain and flour export quotas from 15 June to 1 September.
  • Meanwhile, US farmers with land in the final year of their CPR (conservation reserve program) contract can end their agreements early in a bid to boost crop production from autumn onwards. USDA says 3.4 mln acres are due to expire.
  • Elsewhere, crop prospects appear less rosy. The US attaché in India has cut its estimate of the country’s wheat crop by about 11mln t to 99 mln t, due to intense heat.
  • India has allowed wheat shipments of 469,000t since banning most exports last month, but at least 1.7 mln t is still lying in export facilities and may be at risk from looming monsoon rains.
  • Buenos Aires Grain Exchange has trimmed its estimate of Argentina’s 2022/23 wheat area to 6.5 mln ha, down from 6.6 mln ha, due to dry weather and rapidly depleting soil moisture.
  • France’s wheat crop has continued to deteriorate due to heat, with crop ratings (good or very good condition) slipping to 67% in the week ending 30 May.
  • Following this, severe storms moved across France last weekend. Although too early to for any real damage assessment, the French Farmers’ Union said it was significant and talked of 100% losses for some farms.
  • In addition, France’s farm ministry has cut its estimates of the country’s 2022 soft wheat area to 4.76 mln ha, down from 4.8 mln ha last month.
  • Crop sowing remains well behind in Canada due to adverse weather conditions, causing farmers to revaluate acreage plans.
  • Egypt’s trade ministry has raised the maximum moisture content on wheat imports to 14%, which will open up other origins for international tenders.

Feed Barley

  • Activity in the barley market has picked up over the past week or so following interest for feed barley materialising in the Spanish market, and we have seen several cargoes trade to northern Spain/Portugal in recent days.
  • Activity in the domestic market is still rather quiet, although once again we have seen some consumers taking cover with the weakness in futures.
  • The UK has received beneficial rains over the last week and on the whole crops look to be in good condition having had ample moisture. As of today, we expect to see good yields this harvest.
  • UK farmers are generally fairly well sold compared with the usual pace. For the time being origination is quiet and we do not expect to see much activity from the farmer now until harvest.

Rapeseed

  • Energy markets have made new recent highs, with West Texas Intermediate crude closing over $121 a barrel.
  • The US is still experiencing showers, but normal temperatures are expected going into this weekend before the weather turns hotter and dry towards the end of June. The break in rain for some areas will allow those behind on planting to catch up. The US is reported to be 78% planted now versus 66% last week (89% last year).
  • Soybeans continue to rally, hitting new highs on old and new crop during yesterday’s session, but couldn’t hold over $17.50/bushel. Apparent nearby demand will give some underlying support in the short term.
  • Traders now await tomorrow’s June USDA WASDE report for direction.
  • In South America, exports out of Brazil are slow, with 7.6 mln t left in the line-up. Brazil’s National Supply Company (CONAB) increased the country’s soybean crop estimate to 124.3 mln t as a result of higher yields and a slightly higher area. That still trails USDA’s 125 mln t.
  • Harvest in Argentina is nearly complete. Buenos Aires Grain Exchange reports the country’s harvest at 94% complete. BAGE also increased its crop forecast to 43.3 mln t (+1.3 mln t). There were also rumours of government introducing a tax on profits resulting from the Russia/Ukraine war, but it has to pass congress first.
  • In China, Covid-19 lockdowns are now being relaxed in some areas, which should help the re-opening of businesses. It was reported last week that China cancelled 72,000t of US soybeans. Heavy rains have set in across most of the large rapeseed growing regions of China which will delay harvest, and crop production estimates are being reduced from 6 mln t to closer to 5 mln t.
  • After several sessions of veg oil prices trying to keep up with crude oil, news that Indonesia will increase palm exports through to the end of July has pressured prices today. Malaysian palm traded 5% down this morning.
  • MATIF rapeseed appears rangebound for the past week, struggling to break back up to the highs seen a few weeks ago. EU and UK rapeseed prices have fallen sharply from those highs.

Oats

  • Oat markets have been mixed this week, with good crop conditions in the EU being offset by the continued planting delays or adverse weather in North America. Farmers in the EU are reluctant sellers of new crop given the uncertainty about quality. They are also fully aware of the general global tightness in the oat balance sheet and the reliance on a bounce-back in North American production. If we see a poor Canadian/US harvest, growers could expect higher prices.
  • Here in the UK, crop conditions continue to be favourable, with winter crops rated at 87% good to excellent and springs at 75%. This compares to 78% respectively for both winters and springs last year. The weather currently looks fair. Therefore, we should expect to see average yields and another exportable surplus in 2022/23.
  • Bottom line, it is now all about the weather to determine where oat prices go from here.

Pulses

Beans

  • Old crop demand in the UK is fairly thin. Most consumers are covered for June with gaps to fill between July and August. Given the amount of beans remaining on farm is quite small this is to be expected.
  • New crop feed beans continue to track the Nov 22 LIFFE wheat futures. Very little farmer selling is being reflected in the thin demand from consumers. Production aspects in the three major origins are all looking good at this stage.
  • Australia’s latest crop report for June indicated some positive news for their upcoming crops:
  • Australian national planting to winter crops in 2022–23 is forecast to be the second highest on record at 23.4 mln ha.
  • Winter crop production in 2022–23 is forecast to reach the fourth highest on record at 50.9 mln t.
  • Summer crops production in 2021–22 is estimated to reach a new national record of 5.5 mln t.

Peas

  • Old crop peas are mainly covered across the board. We will have some feed pea availability over the next two positions, so please get in touch if you have any peas remaining on farm.
  • Our inaugural VIPea day is to take place on the 21 June. It provides a great opportunity to learn more about peas and the five key stages of growing the commodity. Please get contact your farm trader for further information.

Seed

  • We have variety demonstration days taking place across the country throughout June. It is not too late to get booked onto a variety day close to you. Contact your farm trader for more information.
  • Why risk not having seed on farm? Duplo OSR from DSV is available on a sale-or-return offer, giving growers flexibility to return up to 70% of their seed if they decide not to drill.
  • OSR companion crops are a great option to help aid the establishment of the oilseed rape. ADM has three great choices which provide several benefits, including fixing nitrogen and deterring flea beetle.
  • ADM also has a range of cover crop straights available with the option to make them into a mix to suit your requirements.
  • See our seed varieties guide for more information on our autumn seed portfolio.

Fertiliser

  • Granular urea in North America (NOLA) continues to drift towards price levels where re-export to South America or Europe becomes viable. Changes in North American cropping and strong supply into the region has left some traders having to rethink positioning of product.
  • Granular urea in the UK remains relatively flat. Quieter demand is holding off any significant gains, whilst UK and imported ammonium nitrate (AN) prices are limiting any downward price movement.
  • ADM has liquid urea ammonium nitrate (UAN) and UAN + ATS grades for open market summer tank fill. These tonnes remain limited, and until clarity on Russian UAN export supplies are realised, UAN prices are expected to remain elevated above historical norms.
  • The announcement from CF regarding the Ince facility yesterday continues to show the difficulties in European production costs and product range availability. Read the announcement here.
  • UK AN prices are currently withdrawn, with limited imported 34.4% and 33.5% products available.
  • ADM has Piamon 33N 30SO3 (a granular urea sulphur compound) available for Sept, Oct, Nov delivery months. With tight supply of high nitrogen sulphur products, this high quality compound is perfect for application on OSR.
  • Phosphate and potash prices continue to sit at all-time highs. The direction of prices for the product after the autumn application season is currently debatable.
  • Demand destruction for potash and phosphates in key regions are highlighting that a downward movement could occur in Q4 2022. This is dependent on Belarussian and Russian sanctions into western markets, amongst other factors.
