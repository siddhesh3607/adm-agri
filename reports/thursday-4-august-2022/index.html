Thursday 4 August 2022

This week: Fertiliser trader, Amy Vickers, looks at one of our exclusive fertiliser products, PIAGRAN pro.

Wheat

  • The US markets continue to be concerned about the domestic weather situation and the state of their spring crops. A 3% drop in the weekly spring wheat crop number and another drop in the forecast output for Argentina all helped to underpin the market. The volatile geo-political situation in the Black Sea also encouraged funds to start buying the market again.
  • There remains a lot of uncertainty around the Ukraine export corridor and we continue to question how realistic it is too consider that the country will be able to see any return to normal export volumes any time soon.
  • The missile attack on Odesa 10 days ago, and the shocking death of the founder of one Ukraine’s biggest grain exporters in another missile attack last weekend, only adds to this uncertainty. There is no doubt that Ukraine exports have the potential to ease the tight situation in the grain S&Ds. However, we are now becoming more concerned about the impact of the hot, dry condition to the developing European corn crop.
  • The weather issues in Europe continue to be an issue, but there are also problems for the developing corn and soybean crops in the US, and a hotter and drier forecast for the northwest Midwest, are likely to be the most important issues on the supply side this week. The next four weeks are still critical for corn production in both the US and Europe, and the market will continue to focus on this. If we do see further downside to corn output, especially in the EU, we could see an increase in wheat consumption at a time when the wheat balance sheet cannot afford it.
  • China has been buying French wheat, along with Pakistan and North Africa. All this keeps the pressure on the S&D. However, what if China cut their demand? It could be a lower import year for China. The domestic Chinese corn crop is report in good order so might this curtail their import requirements?
  • Closer to home. The UK wheat is generally making good progress. Anecdotal estimates suggest the crop is nationally 50-60pct complete now with some in the eastern regions finished, whilst some in the north are only just getting started. Yield reports still suggest a “good average” crop. The only area to watch at this stage is the low proteins, which are very variable, even with the same field, but on average seem to be 0.5-0.7 down on last season.

Malting Barley

  • After climbing last week, malting barley prices have retreated again, with lower futures and reports of good quality being harvested from the spring barley crop. Meanwhile, reports of 10t/ha yields in Denmark are helping to add pressure to values in northern Europe.
  • However, there is some spot tightness in the UK domestic market, particularly for winter barley, where some demand still remains and supply has disappeared.
  • Sellers overall are still cautious, currently eyeing the progress of the Scandinavian harvest. If the quality is poor (there is risk of low nitrogen), then European buyers may wash out FOB commitments and look to replace from the UK, which could tighten the market domestically.

Feed Barley

  • Feed barley markets have quietened considerably in recent weeks as the pressure from the winter harvest subsides and growers focus on harvesting and moving other crops.
  • Destination markets remain devoid of demand, although anecdotally, it is there to be done in both Iberia and Ireland. Buyers remain very cautious of ongoing market volatility.
  • Feed barley looks comfortably competitive in the domestic market compared with wheat and maize across all positions, which should lead to sustained demand going into the winter. However, much like buyers abroad, many consumers are inactive for the time being and keeping risk off the table.
  • Bids for barley in the domestic market are climbing, as spot shorts are met with a lack of supply from origination, however there is very little trade to report.

Rapeseed

  • CBOT soybeans trade sharply lower this week following Friday’s sell-off.
  • Market sentiment remains focused on weather. Showers across the east of the US will help save yields, and although the west remains warm and dry, there is still a chance of showers in the next few weeks.
  • Soybean crop ratings actually improved 1% to 60% good/excellent, which now matches last season’s levels. USDA August report is due next week, but private yield estimates are being reduced by up to 1.5 bushels/acre, circa 3% of the typical yield.
  • USDA has reported a sale of 132,000t of US soybeans to an unknown destination, but nothing new to China.
  • In South America, Brazil’s July soybean exports came in at 7.5 mln t versus 8.6 mln t last year, but very little happening in Argentina.
  • The US visit to Taiwan has caused friction between the US and China, which forces the latter back to buying South American soybeans, even at a premium.
  • Crude oil prices have touched $90/barrel again following reports from the US government reducing its fossil fuel demand. Malaysian palm prices and soy oil prices came under pressure following the fall in energy markets.
  • Canadian rapeseed prices rallied last week, but were pressured by the weaker complex this week. The Canadian Prairies received rain over the weekend, but Alberta and Saskatchewan remained largely dry. Crop ratings in Alberta were reported at 70% good/excellent last week, with yields predicted to be higher than last year.
  • Matif rapeseed closed higher on the week last week, very nearly touch €700/t again on Friday, but prices were pressured this week by the drop in oil values. The EU rapeseed harvest is nearing completion, with bigger yields than expected.

Oats

  • Global oat markets continue to be in a holding pattern, with production in both North America and northern Europe remaining an unknown. The size and quality of both Canadian and Scandinavian crops are crucial in determining market direction. Scandinavian conditions have been largely favourable over the past few weeks and we expect Finland to return to the market as a key exporter this season into western Europe. Consequentially, most European millers won’t be aggressively in the market until they see the quality of Finland’s crop. If Scandinavian supplies are good quality, it is expected that EU prices will be on the defensive. However, with a few weeks still left to go before harvest, there remains a period of likely inertia between the EU buyers and sellers.
  • Canada is still at risk of having lower production than originally forecast due to delayed plantings along with further adverse weather conditions. Therefore, it is still too early for the market to find its true direction.
  • Here in the UK, harvest is well under way, with winter oat quality being generally exceptional. Specific weights are ranging from 47-63kg/hl, with the average at 55.5kg/hl for the samples we have seen through our lab to date. Spring varieties are likely to be much more variable given the severe heat they endured during the key grain fill period.
  • Millers are buyers of spot positions, but the overall consensus by the trade is that there is sufficient supplies of good quality oats to cover demand, but with approximately half the crop being made up of spring varieties, it is too early to conclude whether there is to be a glut of milling quality or whether there is going to be a large volume of feed oats needing a home.
  • Bottom line, the UK market is still awaiting quality and production confirmation before deciding its direction. Therefore, in the short term, oats are following other commodity price moves and harvest pressure in Europe.

Pulses

Beans

  • The bean harvest in the UK is around 5% complete, with some winter beans harvested in the southern half of the UK. Yield reports are variable ranging from 2.5t to 5t/ha, with the average around 4t/ha currently.
  • Only a handful of samples have been seen, but the visual quality of the beans is fairly good, with a reasonably good colour, low levels of staining and no black beans visible. However, insect damage is rather high in most samples seen to date.
  • The market continues to track wheat futures and therefore bean prices are lower on the week. The price of beans is likely to continue to be correlated to the wheat price in the short term.
  • Longer term, we would expect the bean market to disconnect from wheat futures and trade its own supply and demand.
  • Australian-origin beans continue to be the most competitive origin into North Africa. However, there is some demand for early cut European beans at a small premium to feed.

Peas

  • Peas in the UK are at around 60% complete nationally, with many areas expected to be completed over the weekend and into early next week. Rain at the early stages of this week hampered progress.
  • We haven’t had a large amount of samples to reflect this percentage, but from the small amount of samples we have seen in the lab bleaching is very low, with only one sample being over 10%, leading to a lovely green colour.
  • Moistures average at around 14%, which is great given the dryness we experienced over the last month or two. We have also seen cracked seed coat average at around 2%, which is excellent. The peas also do not appear brittle, despite some early fears.
  • Yields range between 2t and 4t/ha, with some large blue peas exceeding the top of that range.
  • We offer competitive pricing for all open market marrowfats. Please get in touch with your farm trader if you have any type of pea on farm that is not on contract.

Seed

  • Two-row winter barleys are still in high demand and seed deliveries are already making their way on to farm. Key varieties such as KWS Tardis are now very limited, so we would advise booking seed as soon as possible to avoid disappointment.
  • With localised rain showers giving many an opportunity to drill OSR, we have a range of varieties available for imminent delivery. If still unsure, why not order DUPLO on our sale and return offer, giving the flexibility to return up to 75%.
  • If you plan on drilling OSR, companion cropping is a great way to help aid establishment. We have three great options available for fast delivery. Contact your farm trader for more information.
  • We also have a range of grass mixes available to suit your requirements, whether that be cutting, grazing, or hay.

Fertiliser

  • Egyptian granular urea prices have continued their upwards movement from last week, increasing by over $100/t, as concerns over future nitrogen fertiliser supply in Europe take effect.
  • Natural gas at the TTF hub in the Netherlands hit a new record on 27 July at €227.50/MWh ($67.70/MMBtu) on the ICE futures exchange. This appears to have stirred Europe and the UK out of harvest focus and into nitrogen buying.
  • Granular urea pricing increased in the UK. With last week’s global movements and strong demand still to surface in Europe, the expectations are for these price rises to continue through the autumn period.
  • European nitrate prices also moved upwards this week, following Yara`s latest offer, who along with all continental producers are struggling to contend with the volatility in gas supplies.
  • The decrease in global sulphur prices continues, although product here in the UK has already been purchased and stored, so it is unlikely we will see an immediate drop at the farm gate.
