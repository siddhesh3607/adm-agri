Wheat

  • The wheat market continues to lack direction, with all wheat futures markets trading within the same range that started back in mid-July. The combination of the weather, geo-politics, and the wider macroeconomic uncertainty, make it a very difficult market to read.
  • The US and Canada are currently seeing above average temperatures, which is aiding the spring wheat harvest, and it is a similar picture in Russia. The rest of Europe will see some showers, but it is still very dry, which is a bit of a concern for winter planting for the 2023 crop, but Australia is getting some good rain and could be setting up for another good year.
  • The export corridor in the Black Sea appears to be holding, with Ukraine reportedly shipping 3 mln t of all grains in July and 4 mln t in August. However, it is worth noting that wheat exports made up a comparatively small percentage of this total volume. Russia has had a bumper harvest and is now being aggressive with offers, so despite EU exports being up 25% compared to last year, we are now expecting to see more switching from northern Europe to Black Sea and this has been reflected in the declines in Matif wheat futures.
  • UK prices have supported by the fall in sterling, which has dropped circa 300 pts in a week. The pound came under pressure as many in the market expect to see inflation rise faster than the BoE is currently forecasting. Goldman Sachs said it expected inflation to peak at 14.8% in January 2023 and that consumption is likely to decline significantly, which would have a major negative impact on UK economic growth.
  • Despite the drop in sterling, we have not seen enough movement in the UK’s export competitiveness to kickstart any significant volume of demand. Russia is doing its best to keep us out of the North Africa market where it can and, whilst there are a few nearby feed wheat cargoes moving to Spain and Ireland, the Danish re-sellers are hindering our progress.
  • In summary, this wheat market remains a tough one to call. Seasonally, wheat can be difficult to buy from the farmer in September, and with demand still there for Q4 onwards, this should underpin values, but with many other ongoing issues, we might continue to trade within the recent £20 range until we get some new, more meaningful news.

Malting Barley

  • Malting barley prices have tracked sideways over the course of the week as farmer selling continues to slow.
  • Premiums against feed barley are squeezing in further, as feed prices remain supported on the week. We still expect prices to come down further, as the UK needs to find export parity to level the balance sheet.
  • Maltsters in the UK are becoming full on their 2022 requirements, leading to a slip in OND prices vs JFM and AMJ. We would expect the deferred positions to follow suit once buying interest slows.

Feed Barley

  • Feed markets have risen on the week, supported by firmer wheat futures.
  • Fundamentally, there is little change to the dynamic on feed barley. In the UK and overseas, feed barley is competitive against wheat and maize in feed rations, which should keep domestic demand in northern Europe supported. As a result, any further upside in futures markets should take barley along for the ride.

Rapeseed

  • November soybeans closed down on the week, but prices are struggling to re-establish a clear trend and markets remain choppy. Domestic US soybean crush reports showed that the volume processed had slipped to nine-month lows. This, combined with some disappointing weekly US export numbers and an optimistic outlook for South American soy production, pushed prices lower.
  • The rapeseed market was negatively impacted by the weaker soy complex. In addition, the prospect of an improved Canadian rapeseed crop and worries about a wider macro-economic slowdown is not helping either. The old crop EU balance for OSR is now looking more comfortable and, unless there is a major unforeseen bullish driver, old-crop rallies are probably selling opportunities.
  • However, crop year 2023 could be a very different matter. It remains very dry throughout a lot of north west Europe and the drilling window is rapid closing. There is a long way to go, but we wouldn’t be surprised to see a drop in European rapeseed availability next season.

Oats

  • Global oat markets have received some fresh fundamental news this week, with StatsCan releasing its latest production figures for Canada’s 2022/23 crop. The overall summary is an estimated 59% bounce in oat production vs last year, to 4.5mln t. This follows an estimated 10.6% rise in the oat-harvested area, along with a 44.1% rise in yields. It must be noted that these figures are in comparison to last year’s crop, which suffered one of the worst droughts in living memory. Therefore, the 18.3% increase in production vs the five-year average is a more comparable figure and should see Canada export about 500,000t more than last year.
  • Further to the Canadian news, harvest is well underway in Finland. Current estimates are that 25-30% of the crop has been cut with 50% likely to be cut by the end of the week. Quality is reportedly very good, but some western areas have suffered from lodging damage, which could affect quality. Overall estimates are for a large recovery in Finnish production and a return to 350-375,000t of exports. Prices in Finland are currently being supported by strong domestic demand and a lack of farmer selling.
  • European markets are starting to show signs of stronger demand. However, bids and offers remain quite wide and we would expect trades to start to take place once end-buyers start coming into the market. Demand into Spain for milling oats will also be a feature for the season following the poor-quality harvest the country suffered this year, with buyers bidding for September positions.
  • Here in the UK, oat markets are not following the rallies being seen in the other grains complex. This is not surprising given the quality of our crop and the expected availability of oats. That said, the exceptional quality achieved this year could make the UK more competitive against Scandinavian oats and this could lead to a strong export programme.
  • Bottom line, the EU market is still awaiting the strong buying interest from consumers. Sellers are waiting in the wings, but the UK will need to be competitive in order to shift its exportable surplus.

Pulses

  • The UK pea harvest is nearing completion. Roughly 90% has been cut nationally, with only a small amount of growers needing to harvest a few more fields.
  • Insect damage remains the main issue this year, with average at just over 1.9%. This is workable at Long Sutton with the state of the art machinery we have to bring this figure down for our customers.
  • We are awaiting some final yield reports, but the current average is 2.86t/ha. This figure is likely to differ in next week’s market report when we gather more information from our farmers.
  • If you have any open market marrowfats on farm, please contact your farm trader. We will still offer no bleaching claims on any parcel available if the grower wishes. Bonuses are to be applied retrospectively when intake results are in for under 10% bleached.
  • Feed pea demand is ramping up both domestically and in Europe, with northern Europe keen buyers in the nearby. Prices levels are available via your farm trader.

Seed

  • With rainfall in the forecast, OSR remains in high demand. Although some of our key OSR varieties are starting to sell out, we still have a range of varieties to offer for immediate delivery. Contact your farm trader for updates on availability and delivery timescales.
  • We have several cover crop mixtures available including Grabber and Winter Revivor suitable for over-winter cover. As well as Sprinter which has great autumn biomass production.
  • There is a range of cover crop straights for fast delivery.
  • We can also offer environmental stewardship mixes and grass seed, including agricultural leys, lawns, and paddock mixes to suit your requirements.

Fertiliser

  • Granular urea FOB Egypt has traded up from $750-765/t at the end of last week to $900/t this week.
  • A large urea import programme for Europe is now under way, and India has also issued a tender to buy 1 mln t for shipment by the end of October, adding further support to pricing.
  • Given the array of announcements of ammonia, nitrate and urea production curtailment in Europe last week, North Africa looks likely to remain a key supplier to Europe through the 2022/23 season.
  • The GBP/USD exchange rate has traded from 1.19 to 1.16 in a two-week period, adding to the expense of importing product.
  • Liquid UAN terms have risen in line with recent urea and ammonium nitrate prices. All nitrogen and nitrogen sulphur grades remain available.
  • TSP and MOP remain stable at present in the UK. However, weakness in North and South American PK markets is something to watch.
