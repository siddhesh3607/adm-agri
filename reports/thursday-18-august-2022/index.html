Wheat

Last week’s USDA report didn’t bring any exciting news for the wheat market as there was nothing particularly contentious and the trade took the revision in its stride.

The key changes for wheat saw higher crop estimates for Russia, Australia, Canada and China and reductions in India and the EU.  Whilst the wheat changes were largely benign, changes to the corn numbers were a bit more dramatic, with sharp reductions forecast for EU and US output.

The recent extreme heat and drought has affected EU corn production and this is creating concern that higher wheat demand will result. Increases in wheat feeding usage and higher wheat inclusion by ethanol manufacturers could put pressure on an already-tight European wheat balance sheet.

Despite the potential for higher wheat demand the market has fallen in sharply in the past week and is now testing the bottom of the eight-week trading range. Talk of higher Russian exports, increasing Ukrainian exports and a backdrop of global recession are all having an impact.

It would seem that the trade is trying to take advantage of the freeing-up of grain movements from Ukraine, with traders trying to switch out of North West Europe origination back into the cheaper Black Sea.

The UK harvest is nearly complete – some 10 days earlier than normal. Yields on some of the later crops, particularly those from good, bodied land have been surprisingly good and we wouldn’t be surprised to see a UK wheat crop of between 15.5-16mln t, perhaps a little bigger than we had initially thought. However, even with these bigger crops the UK remains uncompetitive for export and with the Danish back in the market we are struggling to compete.

UK wheat quality is generally very good, aside from protein variability. Results to date would suggest that proteins on milling wheat are between 0.7 and 1% lower than last year and this is keeping a bid under the Group 1 premiums. However, with more crops finding their way onto lower grades or the feed wheat heap we are seeing a widening in domestic quality premiums.

The short-term trend in the market has been lower, but from a production perspective the EU balance sheets for corn and wheat could suggest a medium-term bullish bias. However, the market has still failed to break out of the £255/£275 range on Nov22 ICE London wheat, so for now we continue to trend sideways.

 

Malting Barley

Domestic malting values are slipping lower as barley is coming to the market and maltsters are filling their pre-Christmas requirements.

With an excellent spring crop, the UK will have a comfortable surplus of malting barley this season, so we expect prices to continue to slip into line with the wider northern EU market.

Prices today are very difficult to pin down with the violent drop in futures. Traders have taken a step back and are waiting for the dust to settle before making further decisions.

Feed Barley

Feed barley prices have come under pressure in the latter half of the week, forced lower by the sharp drop in futures.

Physical markets however remain extremely thin with little supply, and the spread between barley and wheat has narrowed on the week by around £5/t.

Export markets are quiet as demand continues to elude sellers, which won’t be helped by weaker MATIF.

We suspect the market will stay quiet until this latest move in futures is digested.

 

Rapeseed

The US soybean market has come down with a bump over the past week with concerns about the wider economic slowdown and lower crude oil prices creating a more negative backdrop to the market.

Last Friday’s USDA report gave the market a bearish surprise, adding to the negative sentiment. The USDA numbers predicted a bigger 2021/22 ending stock figure on the back of lower US exports and they also upped the yield forecast for the upcoming US crop.

With crude oil prices drifting lower and the negative sentiment from the soy complex, the old crop rapeseed market is also coming under pressure with May23 Matif down circa €50 on the week.

The focus for the European rapeseed market is what happens with plantings and potential production for 2023. The recent drought conditions have not been conducive to rapeseed drilling and establishment, and we have been concerned that there could be a drop in crop size for 2023. But recent rains, and with some more in the five-day forecast, may improve the situation. It is something we need to watch very closely.

In the short term the European and UK rapeseed market looks like it will struggle to get back to the highs unless we see a significant uplift in product demand.

Oats

European oat markets have been very quiet over the course of the last week. Consumers remain relaxed on the expectation of a good crop from Scandinavia but until the sellers from Finland/Sweden come to the market with their known quality it is possible that this market will remain illiquid.

Initial indications suggest that the UK is still competitively priced into western Europe, however a lack of water in the Rhine is adding to the costs for inland consumers.

Here in the UK the oat harvest is nearing its completion with southern suppliers wrapping up their spring oats in record time. Quality on the winters are exceptional with 55kg/hl being considered normal. The springs are however more variable with some varietal differences, but many spring samples are also making the milling grade, but we need to see more samples yet.

Bottom line, the UK market continues await harvest quality and EU buyers remain hesitant until they see strong demand.

Pulses

Beans:

UK bean harvest is approximately 45% complete.

It is largely finished in the south of the country and yields, whilst variable, have been satisfactory overall, average around 3.8-4t/ha so far. Quality is mixed. There are high levels of insect damage in many samples, but, visually beans are fairly good with low levels of staining, no black beans and a reasonable colour.

Spring bean yields however have suffered as a result of the hot and dry weather, with some yields as low as 1t/ha reported and the average to date around 2.5t/ha. Yields are expected to improve as harvest moves north.

Bean prices continue to follow wheat futures and are sharply lower on the week as a result.

Fresh demand remains limited domestically and for export into both Europe and North Africa.

Peas:

UK pea harvest is now over 75% complete, with some lagging behind a tad with the recent rains we have seen, hampering their completion of all fields.

Average yields on marrowfats have improved, with some of the late harvesters having better fortunes than those who cut early. Output ranges from 1.5t to 3.5t/ha.

Large blue peas have yielded much better, ranging from 2-4.5t/ha nationally.

Bleaching on both commodities remains low, with over 90% of peas having under 10% bleached – great news for those who chose our bonuses for lower bleaching contract on marrowfats.

Insect damage appears to be the main issue this year for marrowfats. Despite the dryness, cracked seed coat and brittleness in general does not appear to be a problem.

Marrowfats are beginning to move into our Long Sutton Processing facility please ensure you have submitted samples to our lab to ensure quick movement.

 

Seed

Cereal seed processing is well under way with winter barley production largely finished. Availability is limited across some key varieties so we would urge growers to cover their seed requirements sooner rather than later.

With recent rainfall in some areas, we have a selection of OSR varieties available for immediate dispatch. Please get in touch with your farm trader for a full summary of the varieties we have to offer.

As growers begin to look towards drilling, we have some great options of companion crops to accompany OSR. Our choices of companion crops include ingredients to help deter flea beetle and fix nitrogen resulting in a heightened chance of OSR establishment. These are available for fast delivery.

We have several cover crop mixes and cover crop straights available for fast delivery as well, but please book soon to avoid disappointment as they are selling fast.

There are still good stocks of Countryside Stewardship mixes.

 

Fertiliser

UK AN Manufacturer CF withdrew AN terms this week. Expectations are for another significant rise given spikes in UK and European gas markets.

The European gas benchmark, TTF, is currently trading at levels not seen since the Russian invasion of Ukraine (graph below). This is supportive of higher nitrate prices in the region once again.

Granular urea is currently stable with little change on the week. Firmer prices are expected as we enter the autumn, given the European natural gas and nitrate complex.

TSP and MOP have seen minor corrections of circa 5% in the UK, given weak demand for the products in the UK and currently falling global markets.

Nitrogen/sulphur products remain stable, despite falls in global sulphur prices due to high nitrogen costs. High sulphur-consuming crops in the UK will potentially support current prices also.

Liquid UAN terms will be withdrawn tomorrow (Friday 18th) given changes in European nitrate pricing and potential rises in the UK Urea market.
